Deferred Judgment Revocations

Protecting Your Path to Dismissal

Deferred Judgments in Colorado

A deferred judgment is one of the most valuable resolutions available in Colorado criminal cases. Authorized by C.R.S. § 18-1.3-102, a deferred judgment allows a defendant to plead guilty to a charge but have the judgment of conviction "deferred" while the defendant completes a period of supervision and complies with conditions set by the court. If the defendant successfully completes the deferral period, the guilty plea is withdrawn and the case is dismissed—leaving no conviction on the record. If the defendant fails to comply, the deferral can be revoked, the guilty plea remains, and the defendant is sentenced. At Neiley & Morris, we represent clients facing deferred judgment revocations throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

A deferred judgment revocation is one of the most consequential proceedings in a criminal case because it determines whether the defendant walks away with a clean record or with a criminal conviction that may carry significant penalties. Defending against revocation requires a thorough understanding of the deferral terms, the alleged violations, and the procedural and evidentiary issues that arise in revocation hearings.

Common Deferral Conditions

Deferred judgments typically include a range of conditions tailored to the specific offense and the defendant. Common conditions include:

  • Payment of court costs, fines, fees, restitution, and supervision fees
  • Completion of community service hours
  • Drug and alcohol evaluation and treatment
  • Domestic violence treatment or anger management
  • Sex offender treatment for relevant offenses
  • Mental health evaluation and treatment
  • Random drug and alcohol testing
  • Compliance with all laws and avoidance of new offenses
  • No contact with victims or specific persons
  • Reporting requirements to a probation officer or supervising authority
  • Other conditions specific to the case

The conditions of a deferred judgment can be substantial and difficult to meet. Even well-intentioned defendants can find themselves in violation through missed appointments, failure to complete treatment programs, inability to pay financial obligations, or other compliance failures.

Grounds for Revocation

The prosecution may move to revoke a deferred judgment when there is reason to believe the defendant has violated the conditions. Common grounds for revocation petitions include:

  • New criminal charges: Any new offense, even if not yet adjudicated, can trigger revocation proceedings
  • Failed drug or alcohol tests: Positive tests or failures to appear for testing
  • Treatment failures: Termination from a required treatment program for noncompliance
  • Missed appointments: Failure to attend probation appointments, court appearances, or required treatment sessions
  • Failure to pay: Inability or failure to pay required fines, fees, or restitution
  • Failure to complete community service: Not completing the required community service hours within the deadline
  • Contact violations: Contacting persons whom the defendant was ordered to avoid

The Revocation Hearing

When a revocation petition is filed, the defendant is entitled to a hearing at which the prosecution must prove the alleged violations. The standard of proof in deferred judgment revocation hearings is preponderance of the evidence—lower than the beyond-a-reasonable-doubt standard required at criminal trial. This makes revocation easier to prove than the original charges.

At the hearing, the prosecution presents evidence of the alleged violations, which may include testimony from probation officers, treatment providers, or other witnesses, along with documentary evidence such as test results, treatment records, and incident reports. The defendant has the right to confront witnesses, present evidence, and testify on their own behalf, though many defendants choose not to testify because their statements can be used against them in any related proceedings.

Defending Against Revocation

Strong defenses to revocation petitions typically focus on one or more of the following:

  • Factual disputes: Challenging whether the alleged violation actually occurred
  • Legal sufficiency: Arguing that the alleged conduct does not constitute a violation of the specific deferral terms
  • Inability vs. unwillingness: Particularly for financial conditions, demonstrating that the defendant has been unable to comply despite good faith efforts (which generally cannot be the basis for revocation)
  • Procedural defenses: Identifying due process violations or procedural errors in the revocation process
  • Mitigation: Even when violations are clear, presenting mitigating circumstances that may persuade the court to extend or modify rather than revoke the deferral

Inability to Pay Defenses

One of the most important defenses in many revocation cases involves inability to pay financial conditions. The U.S. Supreme Court held in Bearden v. Georgia (1983) that a court cannot revoke probation (and by extension, a deferred judgment) for failure to pay if the defendant has made bona fide efforts to pay but is unable to do so through no fault of their own. This protection requires the court to make specific findings about the defendant's ability to pay before revoking based on financial conditions.

We help clients gather and present the evidence needed to establish inability to pay, including documentation of income, expenses, employment efforts, and payment attempts. This defense is particularly important for clients who are facing revocation primarily because of unpaid fines or restitution they cannot afford.

Outcomes Other Than Revocation

Even when violations are established, revocation is not the only possible outcome. Courts have discretion to extend the deferral period, modify the conditions, impose additional sanctions short of revocation, or revoke and impose alternative sentences. Skilled advocacy can often persuade courts to choose options that preserve the defendant's opportunity for dismissal even when violations have occurred.

Common alternatives to outright revocation include:

  • Extension of the deferral period to allow more time for compliance
  • Addition of new conditions, such as enhanced treatment or additional community service
  • Modification of existing conditions to make compliance more feasible
  • Imposition of brief jail sanctions while keeping the deferral in place
  • Conversion to a probation sentence rather than imposition of a sentence

Consequences of Revocation

If the deferred judgment is revoked, the guilty plea remains in place and becomes a final judgment of conviction. The court then proceeds to sentencing on the underlying charges. Sentencing options depend on the offense and may include probation, jail, prison, fines, treatment requirements, and other sanctions. The defendant now has a criminal conviction that will appear on background checks and may have collateral consequences for employment, housing, immigration, professional licensing, and other areas.

For these reasons, defending against revocation aggressively—or working out alternatives that avoid revocation—is critical. The difference between successful completion and revocation is the difference between no record and a permanent criminal conviction.
