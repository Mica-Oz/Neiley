Direct File of Juveniles in Adult Court

Direct file is one of the most consequential decisions a prosecutor can make in a juvenile case. When a young person is "direct filed" in adult criminal court, they face the full force of the adult criminal justice system—adult procedures, adult penalties, and the potential for an adult criminal record that will follow them for life. The juvenile loses access to the rehabilitative focus of the juvenile justice system and is instead treated as an adult, with all the consequences that entails. At Neiley & Morris, we defend juveniles facing direct file proceedings throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Direct file cases require immediate, sophisticated legal intervention. The decisions made in the early stages of the case—whether to seek a reverse transfer hearing, how to handle pretrial detention, what plea offers to consider—can determine whether the young person spends years in adult prison or has a meaningful opportunity for rehabilitation in the juvenile system.

Colorado's Direct File Statute

Colorado's direct file authority is governed by C.R.S. § 19-2.5-801. The statute allows the district attorney to file charges against a juvenile in adult criminal court rather than juvenile court when certain conditions are met. The juvenile must be at least 16 years old and charged with a class 1 or class 2 felony, a crime of violence, or certain other serious offenses. For some offenses, juveniles as young as 14 may be direct filed.

The decision to direct file is made by the district attorney, not by a judge. There is no preliminary judicial review of whether direct file is appropriate. Once the prosecutor files in adult court, the juvenile is in the adult system unless and until the case is transferred back to juvenile court through a reverse transfer hearing.

Reverse Transfer Hearings

The juvenile defendant has the right to request a reverse transfer hearing under C.R.S. § 19-2.5-802. At the hearing, the court determines whether the case should be transferred from adult criminal court back to juvenile court. The reverse transfer hearing is one of the most important proceedings in any direct file case, and the result often determines the entire trajectory of the prosecution.

The court considers a comprehensive list of factors at the reverse transfer hearing, including:

  • The age of the juvenile
  • The seriousness of the offense and whether it was committed in an aggressive, violent, or premeditated manner
  • Whether the offense was against persons or property
  • The maturity of the juvenile
  • The juvenile's prior record
  • The juvenile's mental and emotional health
  • The likelihood of the juvenile's rehabilitation by use of facilities and programs available to the juvenile court
  • The interest of the community in the imposition of a punishment commensurate with the gravity of the offense
  • The impact of the offense on the victim

The hearing is essentially a battle over whether the juvenile is "amenable to treatment" in the juvenile system. Strong presentations typically include evidence of the juvenile's family support, school history, mental health needs, capacity for change, and the availability of appropriate programming in the juvenile system.

Building a Reverse Transfer Case

Successful reverse transfer cases require thorough investigation and the involvement of qualified mental health and juvenile justice experts. Common elements of strong reverse transfer presentations include:

  • Psychological evaluation: An evaluation by a qualified clinician that documents the juvenile's mental health, developmental level, and treatment needs
  • Educational records: School records that document the juvenile's academic history, behavioral issues, and any special education needs
  • Family support: Testimony from family members about their commitment to supporting the juvenile through rehabilitation
  • Treatment proposals: Specific proposals for the programs and interventions the juvenile would receive in the juvenile system
  • Adolescent brain development evidence: Expert testimony about the developmental immaturity of adolescents and the unique opportunities for change at this stage of life
  • Mitigating circumstances: Evidence about the circumstances of the offense, the juvenile's role, and any factors that reduce moral culpability

Adolescent Brain Development

Modern neuroscience has established that adolescent brains are still developing throughout the late teen years, particularly in areas governing impulse control, risk assessment, and long-term consequences. This research has been recognized by the U.S. Supreme Court in cases like Roper v. Simmons (2005), Graham v. Florida (2010), and Miller v. Alabama (2012), which limited the application of the most severe penalties to juveniles based on developmental science.

While these decisions did not directly limit direct file, they support the broader principle that juveniles should not be treated identically to adults in the criminal justice system. Reverse transfer hearings provide an opportunity to present this scientific evidence and argue that the juvenile's amenability to treatment is supported by what we know about adolescent development.

Pretrial Detention Issues

Juveniles direct filed to adult court are typically held in adult facilities or in juvenile facilities pending the outcome of the case. Conditions of pretrial detention can be harsh and isolating, particularly for younger defendants. Federal law generally prohibits housing juveniles in adult jails for extended periods, but the specific arrangements vary by jurisdiction.

We work to obtain appropriate pretrial release or to ensure that detained juveniles are housed in suitable facilities. Pretrial detention conditions can affect the juvenile's mental health, educational continuity, and ability to participate in their defense.

Sentencing in Direct File Cases

If a juvenile is convicted in adult court (whether by trial or plea), they face adult sentencing—including potential prison sentences. However, the court has some discretion in sentencing, and Colorado law provides certain alternatives that may be available to direct-filed juveniles, including youthful offender system (YOS) sentences. YOS sentences combine elements of juvenile and adult sentencing and provide for incarceration in a specialized facility with a focus on rehabilitation and education.

Direct-filed juveniles convicted of certain offenses may also be eligible for sentencing as juveniles even after conviction in adult court. The availability of these alternatives depends on the specific offense and circumstances. Our defense strategy in direct file cases always considers the sentencing implications and works to preserve the most favorable sentencing options.

Plea Negotiations

Plea negotiations in direct file cases often involve discussions of whether the case should be returned to juvenile court, what charges the juvenile will plead to, and what sentence will be recommended. Skilled negotiation can sometimes achieve resolutions that include juvenile court adjudication or YOS sentencing rather than adult prison time. The strength of the reverse transfer case often influences the prosecutor's willingness to offer favorable terms.
