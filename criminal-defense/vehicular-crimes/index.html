Vehicular Assault and Vehicular Homicide in Colorado

When a motor vehicle accident causes serious injury or death, the driver may face criminal charges that go far beyond a traffic violation. Vehicular assault and vehicular homicide are among the most serious offenses in Colorado law, carrying potential prison sentences, mandatory driver's license revocation, substantial fines, and lifelong consequences. At Neiley & Morris, we defend individuals charged with vehicular crimes throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

These cases are particularly common in Western Colorado, where mountain roads, winter conditions, and the prevalence of recreational alcohol consumption combine to create elevated risks. Treacherous passes, wildlife crossings, fatigued tourists, and seasonal weather all contribute to serious accidents that can result in criminal charges even when the driver had no intent to harm anyone.

Vehicular Assault Under C.R.S. § 18-3-205

Colorado defines vehicular assault in C.R.S. § 18-3-205. The statute provides two separate paths to a vehicular assault charge. The first applies when a person operates or drives a motor vehicle in a reckless manner and causes serious bodily injury to another person. This is a class 5 felony.

The second applies when a person operates a motor vehicle while under the influence of alcohol, drugs, or both, and the conduct is the proximate cause of serious bodily injury to another person. Notably, this version is a strict liability offense for the underlying DUI element—meaning the prosecution does not need to prove that the driver was reckless or even careless. The mere fact of driving under the influence and causing serious injury is enough. This version is a class 4 felony, more serious than the reckless driving version.

Vehicular Homicide Under C.R.S. § 18-3-106

Vehicular homicide, governed by C.R.S. § 18-3-106, follows a parallel structure. A reckless driving version applies when the operator drives in a reckless manner and the conduct causes the death of another person. This is a class 4 felony.

The DUI version applies when a person operates a motor vehicle under the influence of alcohol, drugs, or both, and the conduct is the proximate cause of another person's death. Like vehicular assault, this version is a strict liability offense as to the underlying impairment. It is classified as a class 3 felony, which carries a potential prison sentence of 4 to 12 years and is subject to Colorado's crime of violence enhancements.

Serious Bodily Injury Defined

For vehicular assault charges, the prosecution must prove "serious bodily injury," which is defined more narrowly than ordinary "bodily injury." Serious bodily injury involves a substantial risk of death, serious permanent disfigurement, protracted loss or impairment of a body part or organ, or fractures to the skull or other bones. Whether an injury rises to this level is often disputed and may require expert medical testimony.

The line between simple bodily injury and serious bodily injury can determine whether a defendant faces a felony or a much less serious charge. We work closely with medical experts to evaluate the extent and nature of injuries claimed by the prosecution.

Reckless Driving as the Predicate

For the non-DUI versions of these offenses, the prosecution must prove reckless driving—a substantially higher standard than ordinary careless driving. Recklessness requires conscious disregard of a substantial and unjustifiable risk. Mere mistakes, momentary inattention, or even significant carelessness generally do not rise to recklessness.

Defending against the reckless element often involves accident reconstruction experts who can analyze the physical evidence to assess the speeds, lines of sight, road conditions, and driving behaviors involved. These analyses can reveal that the accident resulted from circumstances other than reckless driving, or that another driver, road condition, or vehicle defect was a significant factor.

DUI-Based Vehicular Charges

The DUI versions of vehicular assault and vehicular homicide present unique defense challenges because they are strict liability offenses for the impairment element. The prosecution does not need to prove fault, recklessness, or even that the impairment caused the accident in any specific way—only that the driver was impaired and that the conduct caused the injury or death.

However, the prosecution still must prove three things: (1) impairment, (2) operation of the vehicle, and (3) proximate cause. Each of these elements presents potential defense opportunities. Impairment can be challenged through analysis of testing procedures, equipment calibration, and the science of blood alcohol or drug analysis. Causation can be challenged when other factors—weather, road conditions, the behavior of other drivers, or the victim's own conduct—contributed to the injury or death.

Causation Defenses

Causation is often the most defensible element in vehicular crime cases. Even when the driver was clearly impaired or driving recklessly, the prosecution must prove that this conduct was the proximate cause of the injury or death. If the accident would have occurred regardless of the impairment or recklessness—because of icy roads, an unexpected obstacle, the victim's actions, or other factors—the causation element may not be met.

Western Colorado's mountain roads create many situations in which causation is genuinely in dispute. A patch of black ice, a deer crossing the road, a sudden change in weather, or the conduct of another driver may have been the actual cause of the accident, with the defendant's conduct being only a contributing factor. We work with accident reconstruction experts and other specialists to develop and present causation defenses.

License Revocation and Collateral Consequences

Conviction of vehicular assault or vehicular homicide results in mandatory revocation of the driver's license for at least one year (often longer), with extended waiting periods before reinstatement. The collateral consequences also include substantial impact on auto insurance, employment (particularly for jobs requiring a clean driving record), and immigration status for non-citizens.

Civil liability is a separate but related issue. The injured parties or families of deceased victims will often pursue civil claims regardless of the criminal outcome. Cooperation with civil counsel and careful management of statements and discovery in the criminal case is important to protect the defendant's interests in both proceedings.

Sentencing Considerations

Vehicular assault and vehicular homicide carry significant prison sentences and are subject to enhancements when certain aggravating factors are present. Colorado's Crime of Violence statute applies to vehicular homicide and requires the court to sentence within the upper range of the presumptive range and to impose the sentence consecutively to other sentences in many cases.

However, sentencing is also influenced by factors specific to the defendant and the case—including the defendant's history, expressions of remorse, support from the community, treatment efforts, and the views of victims and their families. We work to develop comprehensive sentencing presentations that humanize our clients and present the strongest possible case for mercy.
