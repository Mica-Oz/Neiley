Appellate Review of Colorado Criminal Convictions and Sentences

Criminal Appeals in Colorado

When a Colorado trial court enters a criminal conviction or sentence that is wrong on the law, unsupported by the evidence, or the result of constitutional violations, the defendant has the right to appeal. Criminal appeals are heard by the Colorado Court of Appeals and, in some cases, by the Colorado Supreme Court. These appeals are very different from trial court litigation—they require specialized procedures, focus on legal analysis of the trial record, and depend on persuasive written and oral advocacy. At Neiley & Morris, we represent defendants in criminal appeals throughout Colorado, including cases originating in Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

An appeal is not a retrial. The appellate court does not hear new evidence or re-evaluate witness credibility. Instead, the court reviews the trial record for legal errors, abuses of discretion, and constitutional violations. Understanding what can—and cannot—be raised on appeal is essential to evaluating whether an appeal is worth pursuing.

Right to Appeal in Colorado

In Colorado, criminal defendants have a statutory right to appeal final convictions and sentences. Under C.R.S. § 16-12-101 and the Colorado Rules of Appellate Procedure, a notice of appeal must be filed within 49 days after entry of the order or judgment being appealed. This deadline is strict and rarely extended. Missing the deadline forfeits the right to appeal.

Defendants in custody have specific procedural protections, including the right to court-appointed counsel for direct appeals if they cannot afford an attorney. Defendants who can afford to retain counsel may benefit from selecting an attorney with specific appellate experience rather than relying on trial counsel for the appeal.

What Can Be Appealed

Most final orders in criminal cases are appealable, including:

  • Convictions following trial
  • Convictions following guilty pleas, in limited circumstances (waiver issues, sentencing challenges, jurisdictional issues)
  • Sentences, including challenges to legality, length, and conditions
  • Restitution orders
  • Probation revocations and deferred judgment revocations
  • Denials of post-conviction motions
  • Suppression rulings (in some cases by interlocutory appeal)
  • Habitual offender adjudications and sentencing enhancements

Some issues must be preserved at trial to be raised on appeal. Failure to object to errors at trial generally results in plain error review, which is a much more demanding standard. Identifying preserved versus unpreserved errors is one of the first tasks in evaluating an appeal.

Common Appellate Issues

Common issues in Colorado criminal appeals include:

  • Suppression of evidence: Whether the trial court erred in denying a motion to suppress evidence based on Fourth Amendment violations
  • Self-incrimination issues: Whether statements obtained in violation of Miranda v. Arizona were properly excluded
  • Jury instructions: Whether the jury was properly instructed on the elements of the offense, defenses, and lesser-included offenses
  • Sufficiency of the evidence: Whether the evidence presented was legally sufficient to support the conviction
  • Evidentiary rulings: Whether the trial court properly admitted or excluded evidence
  • Prosecutorial misconduct: Whether improper statements or actions by the prosecutor deprived the defendant of a fair trial
  • Ineffective assistance of counsel: Whether trial counsel's performance fell below constitutional standards (typically raised in post-conviction proceedings rather than direct appeal)
  • Double jeopardy: Whether the defendant was improperly subjected to multiple prosecutions for the same offense
  • Sentencing errors: Whether the sentence imposed was illegal, exceeded the statutory range, or violated sentencing principles
  • Speedy trial violations: Whether the case was tried within the time required by Colorado law and the U.S. Constitution

Standards of Review

Different issues are reviewed under different standards on appeal. Understanding the applicable standard is essential to assessing the strength of an appeal.

De novo review applies to questions of law, including constitutional questions and statutory interpretation. The appellate court gives no deference to the trial court's legal conclusions. This is the most favorable standard for appellants.

Abuse of discretion review applies to many evidentiary and procedural rulings. The appellate court will reverse only if the trial court's decision was manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair.

Sufficiency of the evidence review requires the appellate court to view the evidence in the light most favorable to the prosecution and determine whether any rational trier of fact could have found the elements of the offense beyond a reasonable doubt. This is a deferential standard that favors affirmance.

Plain error review applies to issues that were not preserved at trial. The appellate court will reverse only if the error was obvious and substantial and seriously affected the fairness, integrity, or public reputation of the judicial proceedings.

Harmless error analysis applies to constitutional and non-constitutional errors that have been preserved. Even if error is found, reversal is required only if the error was not harmless beyond a reasonable doubt (for constitutional errors) or did not substantially influence the verdict (for non-constitutional errors).

The Appellate Process

Criminal appeals begin with the notice of appeal filed within 49 days of the conviction or order being appealed. After the notice of appeal is filed, the parties prepare and file the record on appeal, which includes the trial court file and transcripts of all relevant hearings. The defendant (now the appellant) files an opening brief that identifies the issues, presents the legal arguments, and cites the authorities. The prosecution (the appellee) files an answer brief, and the appellant may file a reply brief. The court may schedule oral argument or decide the case on the briefs alone.

The Court of Appeals then issues a written decision that may affirm, reverse, or modify the trial court's judgment. If reversed, the case is typically remanded to the trial court for further proceedings—which may include a new trial, resentencing, or other relief. The losing party may petition the Colorado Supreme Court for certiorari review, but the Supreme Court accepts only a small percentage of cases.

Post-Conviction Remedies

Beyond direct appeals, Colorado law provides several post-conviction remedies. Under Colorado Rule of Criminal Procedure 35, defendants can file motions to vacate, set aside, or modify sentences and convictions on various grounds. These include:

  • Crim. P. 35(a): Motions to correct an illegal sentence
  • Crim. P. 35(b): Motions for reduction of sentence within 126 days
  • Crim. P. 35(c): Motions for post-conviction relief based on constitutional violations, ineffective assistance of counsel, newly discovered evidence, and other grounds

Post-conviction motions are typically filed in the trial court but may be appealed to the Court of Appeals if denied. These proceedings provide important protections for defendants whose claims could not be raised on direct appeal—particularly ineffective assistance of counsel claims that depend on evidence outside the trial record.

Sentencing Appeals

Sentencing decisions are subject to appeal on various grounds, including illegal sentences, improper application of sentencing enhancements, errors in restitution calculations, and abuse of discretion in sentencing. The standard of review depends on the specific issue, with some issues reviewed de novo and others reviewed for abuse of discretion. Sentencing appeals can result in resentencing or remand for further proceedings.

Cost-Benefit Considerations

Criminal appeals are time-consuming and require significant legal analysis. The appellate process typically takes a year or more from the notice of appeal to the final decision. Before pursuing an appeal, clients should evaluate the strength of the legal issues, the probability of success, the potential outcome if successful, and the costs involved. We provide candid assessments of appellate prospects so clients can make informed decisions.

Even unsuccessful appeals can have value when they preserve issues for federal habeas corpus review or post-conviction proceedings. Some issues must be raised on direct appeal or they are procedurally defaulted, so the decision to pursue an appeal sometimes depends on preservation considerations as well as the immediate likelihood of success.
