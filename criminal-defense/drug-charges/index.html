Drug Charges Defense

Defending Against Drug Possession, Distribution & Manufacturing Charges

Drug Charges Defense Attorneys in Western Colorado

Drug charges in Colorado range from minor possession offenses to serious felonies carrying lengthy prison sentences. Colorado's drug laws have evolved significantly in recent years, with changes to marijuana regulation, reclassification of certain possession offenses, and evolving enforcement priorities. Navigating this complex legal landscape requires an attorney who understands current Colorado drug law and its practical application in Western Slope courtrooms. At Neiley & Morris, we defend clients facing drug charges throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Our boutique firm provides the focused, personal attention that drug cases demand. Your attorney will review every piece of evidence, challenge unlawful searches, question lab results, and develop a defense strategy specifically tailored to your case and circumstances.

Colorado Drug Possession Laws

Colorado significantly reformed its drug possession laws with the passage of HB 19-1263, which reclassified simple possession of most controlled substances from a felony to a misdemeanor. Under current law, possession of up to 4 grams of a Schedule I or II controlled substance (such as cocaine, heroin, or methamphetamine) is a level 1 drug misdemeanor, punishable by up to 364 days in jail and fines up to $1,000. Possession of more than 4 grams remains a level 4 drug felony with presumptive sentencing of 6 to 12 months in the Department of Corrections.

Marijuana possession follows different rules due to Colorado's legalization framework. Adults 21 and older may possess up to 2 ounces of marijuana. However, possession of more than 2 ounces, possession by minors, and possession on federal land (including national forests and ski areas throughout Western Colorado) remain criminal offenses. Public consumption of marijuana is also illegal and can result in fines and penalties.

Possession of drug paraphernalia is a drug petty offense under C.R.S. § 18-18-428, carrying fines up to $100. However, paraphernalia charges are often filed alongside more serious drug charges and can be used as evidence of intent to use controlled substances.

Drug Distribution and Manufacturing

Distribution, manufacturing, and possession with intent to distribute are far more serious than simple possession charges. Colorado classifies drug distribution offenses based on the type and quantity of substance involved. Distribution of Schedule I or II controlled substances is a level 2 drug felony carrying 4 to 8 years in the Department of Corrections. Distribution to a minor or distribution within 1,000 feet of a school adds sentencing enhancers that can significantly increase penalties.

Manufacturing or compounding controlled substances carries similar penalties. Operating or maintaining a clandestine drug laboratory is a level 1 drug felony—the most serious drug charge in Colorado—carrying 8 to 32 years in the Department of Corrections. Even possession of precursor chemicals with intent to manufacture can result in serious felony charges.

Prosecutors often charge possession with intent to distribute based on circumstantial evidence such as the quantity of drugs, packaging materials, scales, large amounts of cash, multiple cell phones, and statements made to law enforcement. Challenging the inference of intent is a critical component of defending against distribution charges.

Marijuana-Specific Offenses

Despite legalization for recreational use, numerous marijuana-related offenses remain in Colorado law. Unlicensed cultivation of more than 6 plants (or more than 12 plants per household) is a drug felony. Distribution of marijuana outside the regulated system—even between adults—can result in criminal charges. Driving under the influence of marijuana (DUID) is prosecuted aggressively, with a permissible inference of impairment at 5 nanograms of THC per milliliter of blood.

Concentrated marijuana (hash oil, wax, shatter) is treated differently than flower marijuana under certain circumstances. Manufacturing concentrated marijuana using dangerous extraction methods can result in felony charges, including charges related to explosions or fires that occasionally result from butane extraction processes. Our attorneys understand the nuances of Colorado's marijuana laws and defend clients against charges that arise from the complex regulatory framework.

Federal Drug Charges

Western Colorado includes significant federal lands—national forests, BLM land, and national monuments—where federal law applies. Marijuana possession on federal land remains a federal offense regardless of Colorado's legalization. Drug offenses committed on federal property, across state lines, or involving federal investigations can result in federal charges carrying mandatory minimum sentences that are often more severe than state penalties.

Federal drug cases are prosecuted in the U.S. District Court for the District of Colorado, and the procedural rules, sentencing guidelines, and plea negotiation process differ substantially from state courts. Our attorneys have experience in federal court and understand the distinct challenges federal drug cases present.

Search and Seizure Challenges

Many drug cases hinge on whether law enforcement obtained evidence through a lawful search. The Fourth Amendment protects individuals from unreasonable searches and seizures, and evidence obtained in violation of this right can be suppressed—often resulting in dismissal of charges. Common search and seizure issues in drug cases include traffic stops without reasonable suspicion, searches of vehicles, homes, or persons without a warrant or valid exception, consent obtained through coercion or deception, prolonged traffic stops that exceed their lawful scope, and reliance on confidential informants whose reliability has not been established.

In Western Colorado, drug stops frequently occur on Interstate 70, Highway 82, and other major thoroughfares. Law enforcement officers may profile vehicles or use drug detection dogs during routine traffic stops. Our attorneys carefully analyze the circumstances of every traffic stop and search to identify potential constitutional violations that could result in suppression of evidence.

Drug Courts and Diversion Programs

Colorado offers drug court programs and diversion alternatives for eligible defendants, particularly those whose offenses are driven by substance abuse. Drug court provides structured treatment, regular court appearances, drug testing, and supervision as an alternative to incarceration. Successful completion of drug court typically results in dismissal of charges or reduced sentences.

Eligibility for drug court varies by judicial district and depends on factors including the nature of the charges, criminal history, and willingness to participate in treatment. Our attorneys evaluate whether drug court or diversion is an appropriate option for each client and advocate for admission into these programs when they serve the client's best interests.

DUID – Driving Under the Influence of Drugs

Driving under the influence of drugs (DUID) is charged under the same statute as alcohol-related DUI—C.R.S. § 42-4-1301. Unlike alcohol DUI, there is no per se legal limit for most drugs. Instead, prosecutors must prove that the driver was substantially incapable of safely operating a vehicle due to drug consumption. For marijuana, there is a permissible inference of impairment at 5 nanograms of THC, but this inference can be rebutted with evidence. Consequences Beyond Criminal Penalties

Drug convictions carry collateral consequences that extend far beyond fines and incarceration. A drug conviction can result in loss of federal financial aid eligibility, suspension or revocation of professional licenses, immigration consequences including deportation for non-citizens, loss of public housing eligibility, difficulty finding employment due to background checks, and suspension of driving privileges. Our attorneys consider these collateral consequences when developing defense strategy and negotiating with prosecutors, working to minimize the long-term impact of drug charges on your life and career.

Prescription Drug Offenses

Prescription drug offenses have become an increasingly significant area of drug law enforcement. Charges can arise from possessing prescription medications without a valid prescription, obtaining prescriptions through fraud or doctor shopping, distributing or sharing prescription medications, and healthcare provider fraud involving controlled substance prescriptions. These cases often involve complex medical and pharmaceutical evidence that requires careful analysis and expert testimony to effectively defend.
