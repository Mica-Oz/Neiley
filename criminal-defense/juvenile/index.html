Juvenile Defense

Protecting Young People's Rights and Futures

Juvenile Defense Attorneys in Western Colorado

When a young person faces criminal charges, the stakes extend far beyond the immediate legal consequences. A juvenile record can affect college admissions, scholarship eligibility, military service, employment, and a young person's sense of self at a formative stage of life. Colorado's juvenile justice system is designed to be rehabilitative rather than punitive, but navigating it effectively requires experienced legal counsel. At Neiley & Morris, we defend juveniles facing delinquency proceedings throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Our attorneys approach juvenile cases with the understanding that young people are still developing and that the goal of the justice system should be accountability balanced with rehabilitation. We work directly with families to understand the circumstances that led to the charges and advocate for outcomes that protect the young person's future.

Colorado's Juvenile Justice System

Colorado's juvenile justice system, governed by the Children's Code (Title 19, Colorado Revised Statutes), operates differently from the adult criminal justice system. Juveniles aged 10 through 17 who are accused of criminal offenses are generally processed through the juvenile court system, where proceedings are called "delinquency" cases rather than criminal cases. A finding of delinquency results in an "adjudication" rather than a "conviction," and dispositions focus on rehabilitation, accountability, and community safety.

Juvenile court proceedings are generally closed to the public, and juvenile records are subject to confidentiality protections that do not apply to adult criminal records. However, there are exceptions for serious offenses, and certain juvenile records can become public or be used in subsequent adult proceedings. Understanding these protections and their limitations is essential for families navigating the juvenile system.

Common Juvenile Offenses

Juveniles in Western Colorado face a wide range of charges, many of which arise from the unique characteristics of mountain resort communities. Common offenses include minor in possession of alcohol (MIP), which is particularly prevalent in resort towns where alcohol is abundant at events and gatherings. Drug possession charges, including marijuana possession by minors, are also common despite Colorado's adult legalization.

Theft and shoplifting charges frequently arise in resort retail environments. Assault and fighting charges may stem from school incidents, sporting events, or social situations. Vandalism and criminal mischief charges can result from youthful behavior that crosses legal lines. Trespassing charges may involve accessing closed ski terrain, private property, or construction sites. Sexting and technology-related offenses have become increasingly common as young people navigate the digital landscape without understanding the legal implications of sharing certain content.

Rights of Juveniles in the Justice System

Juveniles in Colorado have many of the same constitutional rights as adults, including the right to remain silent, the right to an attorney, the right to confront witnesses, and the right against unreasonable search and seizure. However, there are important differences. Juveniles do not have the right to a jury trial in Colorado—juvenile cases are decided by a judge. Juveniles also have the right to have a parent or guardian present during questioning, and statements made without proper advisement may be suppressed.

The right to counsel is particularly important in juvenile cases. Research shows that juveniles are more susceptible to coercive interrogation techniques, more likely to waive their rights without understanding the consequences, and more likely to make false confessions. Having an attorney present from the earliest stages of the process helps protect against these vulnerabilities.

Diversion and Alternative Programs

Colorado strongly favors diversion and alternative programs for juvenile offenders, particularly first-time offenders and those charged with less serious offenses. Diversion programs allow juveniles to complete requirements such as community service, counseling, educational programs, and restitution without formal court adjudication. Successful completion results in dismissal of the charges.

Restorative justice programs are available in many Colorado communities and focus on repairing harm rather than imposing punishment. These programs bring together the juvenile, the victim, and community members to develop an accountability plan. Teen court programs allow juvenile offenders to be heard by a jury of their peers. Community service, mentoring programs, and substance abuse education are other alternatives to formal adjudication.

Our attorneys evaluate every available alternative and advocate for diversion and rehabilitative options when they serve the young person's best interests. Keeping a juvenile out of the formal court system, when appropriate, provides the best opportunity for long-term success.

Direct File to Adult Court

In certain circumstances, Colorado law allows the district attorney to "direct file" charges against a juvenile in adult criminal court. Direct file is available for juveniles aged 14 and older who are charged with certain serious felonies, including class 1 and class 2 felonies, and for juveniles aged 16 and older charged with certain violent felonies. When a case is direct filed, the juvenile faces adult penalties, adult criminal procedures, and the possibility of an adult criminal record.

Direct file cases are among the most serious situations a young person can face in the criminal justice system. The juvenile has the right to request a "reverse transfer" hearing where the court determines whether the case should be returned to juvenile court. Factors the court considers include the seriousness of the offense, the juvenile's maturity and sophistication, the juvenile's prior record, the likelihood of rehabilitation within the juvenile system, and the interests of community safety.

Our attorneys vigorously advocate for reverse transfer when appropriate, presenting evidence of the juvenile's rehabilitative potential, family support, and capacity for change. Keeping a young person in the juvenile system, when possible, provides access to age-appropriate interventions and avoids the lasting consequences of an adult criminal record.

Sentencing and Disposition in Juvenile Cases

If a juvenile is adjudicated delinquent, the court enters a disposition (sentence) that may include probation with conditions such as counseling, community service, and curfews, commitment to the Division of Youth Services (DYS) for serious offenses, placement in a residential treatment facility, restitution to the victim, participation in specific treatment or education programs, and community-based supervision.

Commitment to the Division of Youth Services is reserved for the most serious offenses and represents the juvenile equivalent of incarceration. DYS commitment can last up to the juvenile's 21st birthday for serious offenses, though many commitments are shorter. Even when commitment is imposed, the focus remains on rehabilitation, education, and preparing the young person for successful reentry into the community.

School Discipline and Juvenile Charges

Juvenile criminal charges often arise from incidents at school and can trigger both criminal proceedings and school disciplinary action. Expulsion, suspension, and alternative school placement may result from criminal charges regardless of the outcome of the criminal case. Colorado law governs when schools can expel or suspend students and provides due process protections including notice, a hearing, and the right to appeal.

Coordinating the defense of criminal charges with school discipline proceedings requires careful strategy. Statements made in one proceeding can affect the other, and the outcomes are interconnected. Our attorneys help families navigate both systems simultaneously, protecting the young person's educational opportunities while defending against criminal charges.

Juvenile Records and Expungement

Colorado law provides for the expungement (sealing) of juvenile records in certain circumstances. Juvenile records may be expunged after the case is dismissed, after successful completion of a diversion program, after completion of a sentence or disposition, or after a specified waiting period depending on the offense. Expungement effectively erases the record, allowing the juvenile to honestly state that they have not been arrested or adjudicated for the expunged offense.

However, not all juvenile records are eligible for expungement. Certain serious offenses, including sex offenses requiring registration and offenses that would constitute crimes of violence if committed by an adult, may not be eligible. Our attorneys advise families on expungement eligibility and timing, and file expungement petitions when the young person qualifies.
