Theft Defense

Defending Against Theft, Shoplifting & Property Crime Charges

Theft Defense Attorneys in Western Colorado

Theft charges in Colorado encompass a wide range of offenses from minor shoplifting to serious felonies involving substantial financial loss. Regardless of the value involved, a theft conviction creates a criminal record that labels you dishonest—a designation that can follow you for life and affect employment, housing, professional licensing, and personal relationships. At Neiley & Morris, we provide vigorous defense for individuals facing theft-related charges throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Our attorneys understand that theft charges often arise from misunderstandings, mistakes, or difficult personal circumstances. We approach every case without judgment, focusing on building the strongest possible defense to protect your rights and your future.

Colorado Theft Classifications

Colorado classifies theft offenses primarily by the value of the property or services involved. Under C.R.S. § 18-4-401, theft occurs when a person knowingly obtains, retains, or exercises control over anything of value belonging to another without authorization or by threat or deception, and intends to permanently deprive the owner of its use or benefit.

Petty theft involving property valued at less than $300 is a petty offense carrying fines but no jail time. Theft of property valued between $300 and $750 is a class 2 misdemeanor with up to 120 days in jail. Theft of property between $750 and $2,000 is a class 1 misdemeanor carrying up to 364 days in jail. Theft of property valued between $2,000 and $5,000 is a class 6 felony with 12 to 18 months in the Department of Corrections. Values between $5,000 and $20,000 constitute a class 5 felony, between $20,000 and $100,000 a class 4 felony, between $100,000 and $1,000,000 a class 3 felony, and theft of property exceeding $1,000,000 is a class 2 felony carrying 8 to 24 years.

Shoplifting and Retail Theft

Shoplifting is one of the most commonly charged theft offenses, particularly in Western Colorado's resort communities where high-end retail stores in Telluride and Aspen attract tourists and seasonal visitors. Shoplifting charges can arise from concealing merchandise, switching price tags, under-ringing items at self-checkout, or removing security devices.

Retailers in Colorado also have the right to pursue civil recovery in addition to criminal charges. Under Colorado's civil theft statute, a merchant can demand payment of the retail value of merchandise plus a penalty. Responding appropriately to both the criminal and civil aspects of a shoplifting case requires experienced legal guidance.

Many shoplifting charges can be resolved through diversion programs, deferred judgments, or plea negotiations that avoid a theft conviction on your record. Our attorneys evaluate every option for keeping your record clean, particularly for first-time offenders and individuals whose livelihoods depend on passing background checks.

Burglary

Burglary in Colorado is defined as knowingly entering or remaining unlawfully in a building or occupied structure with the intent to commit a crime therein. Colorado distinguishes between three degrees of burglary. First-degree burglary under C.R.S. § 18-4-202 involves entering a dwelling and assaulting or menacing a person inside—this is a class 3 felony and a crime of violence carrying mandatory imprisonment.

Second-degree burglary under C.R.S. § 18-4-203 involves entering a building or occupied structure (not just a dwelling) with intent to commit a crime. This is a class 4 felony when the building is a dwelling and a class 5 felony for other buildings. Third-degree burglary under C.R.S. § 18-4-204 involves breaking into a vault, safe, cash register, coin machine, or similar device. This is a class 5 felony.

Burglary charges are more serious than simple theft because they involve unauthorized entry. Even if nothing is taken, the act of entering with criminal intent constitutes burglary. Defenses may include lack of intent, consent to enter, or challenging the evidence that the defendant was the person who entered the structure.

Robbery

Robbery combines theft with the use or threatened use of force. Simple robbery under C.R.S. § 18-4-301 involves taking anything of value from a person by the use of force, threats, or intimidation. This is a class 4 felony. Aggravated robbery under C.R.S. § 18-4-302 involves robbery with a deadly weapon, causing injury, or using force that could cause serious bodily injury. Aggravated robbery is a class 3 felony and an extraordinary risk crime, carrying enhanced penalties.

Robbery charges are among the most serious property crimes in Colorado and are often classified as crimes of violence, triggering mandatory prison sentences. Defending against robbery charges requires careful analysis of witness identification, the circumstances of the alleged use of force, and any forensic or surveillance evidence.

Identity Theft and Fraud

Identity theft under C.R.S. § 18-5-902 involves using another person's personal identifying information without authorization for any unlawful purpose. Colorado classifies identity theft as a class 4 felony, and when the victim is elderly or disabled, it becomes a class 3 felony. Related charges include criminal impersonation, forgery, fraud by check, and computer crime.

Financial fraud cases often involve complex documentary evidence including bank records, electronic transactions, and digital forensics. Building an effective defense requires careful analysis of this evidence, often with the assistance of forensic accountants or technology experts. Common defenses include lack of knowledge, authorization, mistake of fact, and challenging the reliability of digital evidence.

Embezzlement and Employee Theft

Embezzlement—theft by a person entrusted with another's property—is prosecuted under Colorado's general theft statute but carries particular significance for professionals and employees. Embezzlement charges can arise from misappropriation of employer funds, diverting client or customer payments, unauthorized use of company credit cards, manipulating financial records, and theft of trade secrets or proprietary information.

Embezzlement cases are often investigated for extended periods before charges are filed, meaning the accused may be unaware of the investigation. If you suspect you are under investigation for employee theft or embezzlement, consulting an attorney early—before charges are filed—can significantly impact the outcome.

Motor Vehicle Theft

Aggravated motor vehicle theft is charged separately from general theft under C.R.S. § 18-4-409. First-degree aggravated motor vehicle theft is a class 5 felony involving theft of a vehicle valued at $20,000 or more, theft achieved through force or threats, or theft from a motor vehicle dealer. Second-degree aggravated motor vehicle theft is a class 1 misdemeanor for vehicles valued under $20,000. Joyriding—taking a vehicle without intent to permanently deprive the owner—is treated differently from theft with intent to keep or sell the vehicle.

Defenses to Theft Charges

Effective theft defenses depend on the specific facts of each case. Common defenses include lack of intent to permanently deprive—you must intend to keep the property, not merely borrow it. Claim of right is a defense when you genuinely believed you had a right to the property. Consent of the owner negates the element of unauthorized taking. Mistaken identity is particularly relevant in cases relying on surveillance footage or eyewitness identification. Duress may apply when the theft was committed under threat of harm.

Additionally, challenging the value of the property can reduce the severity of charges. If the prosecution cannot prove the property's value beyond a reasonable doubt at the threshold for a more serious charge, the offense may be reduced to a lesser classification.

Consequences of a Theft Conviction

Beyond incarceration and fines, theft convictions carry significant collateral consequences. A theft conviction brands you as dishonest, which can be devastating for employment prospects, professional licensing, and personal reputation. Specific consequences include mandatory restitution to the victim, potential loss of professional licenses for attorneys, accountants, financial professionals, and others in positions of trust, immigration consequences for non-citizens, difficulty passing background checks for employment and housing, and a permanent criminal record unless eligible for sealing.

Colorado does allow sealing of certain criminal records, but theft convictions have specific waiting periods and eligibility requirements. Our attorneys help clients understand their options for minimizing the long-term impact of theft charges, including pursuing dispositions that may be eligible for future record sealing.
