Colorado Parks and Wildlife Violations

Western Colorado is one of the most popular hunting, fishing, and outdoor recreation destinations in the country. Big game hunters travel from across the nation to pursue elk, deer, bear, and other species in the high country surrounding Aspen, Telluride, and Rifle. Anglers fish the Roaring Fork, Crystal, Frying Pan, and other legendary rivers. Off-highway vehicle enthusiasts explore thousands of miles of public lands. With this volume of outdoor activity comes regulatory enforcement—and sometimes criminal charges. At Neiley & Morris, we defend hunters, anglers, and outdoor recreationists facing Parks and Wildlife violations throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Wildlife violations may seem minor compared to other criminal charges, but the consequences can be severe and long-lasting. License revocations, large fines, restitution payments for the value of wildlife, and even jail time are possible. Hunters and outfitters can lose their ability to participate in their sport for years or permanently. Out-of-state visitors may face complications with their hunting licenses in their home states.

Common Wildlife Charges

Colorado Parks and Wildlife (CPW) enforces a complex regulatory scheme codified primarily in Title 33 of the Colorado Revised Statutes. Common charges include:

  • Illegal taking or possession of wildlife: Hunting without a license, hunting in a closed area, hunting outside the season, taking the wrong species or sex, exceeding bag limits, or taking wildlife by unlawful means
  • Improper tagging: Failing to properly tag harvested game, sharing tags between hunters, or other tagging violations
  • Wanton waste: Failing to properly care for and salvage edible portions of harvested game
  • Spotlighting: Using artificial lights to take or attempt to take big game
  • Trespass while hunting or fishing: Entering private property to hunt or fish without permission
  • Outfitting violations: Operating without proper outfitter registration or violating outfitter regulations
  • Fishing violations: Fishing without a license, exceeding limits, taking protected species, or using prohibited methods
  • OHV violations: Operating off-highway vehicles in closed areas, on closed routes, or without proper registration

Misdemeanor and Felony Wildlife Crimes

Most wildlife violations are misdemeanors or petty offenses, but some can be charged as felonies. The most serious wildlife offenses involve illegal taking of trophy animals or commercial activity. Under C.R.S. § 33-6-109 and related statutes, knowingly taking or possessing certain trophy big game animals can result in significant criminal penalties and enhanced license suspensions.

Samson's Law, named after a notable trophy elk killed illegally in 1995, increased penalties for the illegal taking of trophy-class wildlife. The law allows enhanced fines based on the size and quality of the animal, sometimes reaching tens of thousands of dollars in addition to other penalties.

License Suspension and Wildlife Hearings

One of the most significant consequences of a wildlife conviction is the potential loss of hunting and fishing privileges. CPW can suspend hunting and fishing licenses through administrative proceedings that are separate from the criminal case. The Wildlife Hearings process allows CPW to suspend privileges for various violations, with suspension periods ranging from one year to lifetime.

The administrative hearing process has its own procedures, evidentiary standards, and appeal mechanisms. Even if criminal charges are dismissed or reduced, the administrative consequences may proceed. We represent clients in both the criminal cases and the administrative hearings to ensure that all dimensions of the consequences are addressed.

Wildlife Restitution

Colorado law allows the state to seek restitution for the value of wildlife unlawfully taken. Restitution amounts are set by statute and can be substantial, particularly for big game species. Bull elk, mule deer bucks, mountain lions, bears, and other species carry restitution values that can total thousands or tens of thousands of dollars. Restitution is generally separate from any fines imposed in the criminal case and from any administrative consequences.

Interstate Wildlife Enforcement

Colorado is a member of the Interstate Wildlife Violator Compact (IWVC), which means that wildlife violations and license suspensions in Colorado are typically reported to and honored by other member states. A hunter from another state who is convicted of a wildlife violation in Colorado may find that they cannot hunt or fish anywhere in the IWVC member states. This makes the consequences of a Colorado wildlife conviction far more significant than they might initially appear.

The interstate dimension of wildlife enforcement also means that wildlife violations elsewhere can affect Colorado hunting privileges, and vice versa. Out-of-state hunters facing charges in Colorado should give serious attention to defending the case rather than simply paying fines and moving on.

Defending Wildlife Cases

Wildlife cases often involve fact-intensive disputes about exactly what happened, where it happened, and what the hunter knew or intended. Common defense issues include:

  • Proof of location: Whether the hunting or fishing actually occurred in a closed area, on private property, or outside the licensed area
  • Identification of species or sex: Whether the harvested animal was actually the species or sex prohibited
  • Knowledge and intent: Whether the hunter knew they were violating the law or made a good-faith mistake
  • Search and seizure issues: Whether wildlife officers conducted lawful stops and searches
  • Chain of custody: Whether the evidence (often the harvested animal) was properly handled and preserved

We work with our clients to thoroughly investigate the facts and identify the strongest available defenses.

Outfitters and Guides

Outfitters and hunting guides face additional regulatory requirements and enhanced consequences for violations. The Colorado Outfitters Registration Act establishes registration requirements, insurance obligations, and standards of conduct for outfitters. Violations can result in registration suspension or revocation in addition to criminal penalties. Because outfitting is often the outfitter's livelihood, the consequences of a violation can be devastating.

We represent outfitters and guides in defending against violations and in navigating the registration process. Protecting an outfitter's ability to continue operating requires strategic handling of both the criminal case and the regulatory matters.
