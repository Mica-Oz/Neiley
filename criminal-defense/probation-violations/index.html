Probation Violations

Defending Your Freedom When Probation Is at Risk

Probation Violation Attorneys in Western Colorado

A probation violation can transform what was originally a manageable sentence into incarceration. When you are accused of violating the terms of your probation, the stakes are extremely high—the court can revoke probation entirely and impose the original sentence, including prison time. At Neiley & Morris, we provide experienced defense for individuals facing probation violation proceedings throughout Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

Probation violation hearings have different rules than criminal trials, and the consequences can be just as severe. Having an attorney who understands the probation revocation process and can advocate effectively on your behalf is essential to protecting your freedom.

Understanding Probation in Colorado

Probation is a court-supervised alternative to incarceration that allows individuals to remain in the community while serving their sentence. Colorado probation typically includes conditions such as regular reporting to a probation officer, maintaining employment, abstaining from alcohol and drugs with random testing, completing community service hours, paying fines, court costs, and restitution, attending treatment programs including substance abuse counseling, anger management, or domestic violence classes, avoiding contact with victims or co-defendants, not possessing firearms, remaining within the jurisdiction, and complying with all laws.

The specific conditions of probation are tailored to the offense and the individual. Some probation terms include intensive supervision, electronic monitoring, or residential treatment requirements. Understanding every condition of your probation is critical—violating even a minor condition can trigger revocation proceedings.

Types of Probation Violations

Probation violations generally fall into two categories. Technical violations involve breaking a condition of probation without committing a new crime. Common technical violations include missing a scheduled meeting with your probation officer, failing a drug or alcohol test, failing to complete required treatment or community service, leaving the jurisdiction without permission, failing to pay fines or restitution on schedule, and missing curfew or violating electronic monitoring requirements.

Substantive violations involve committing a new criminal offense while on probation. A new arrest triggers probation violation proceedings regardless of whether the new charges ultimately result in conviction. The probation violation case proceeds independently of the new criminal case, and a finding of violation in the probation case does not require proof beyond a reasonable doubt—only a preponderance of the evidence.

The Revocation Process

Probation revocation proceedings begin when the probation officer files a complaint with the court alleging specific violations. The court then issues a warrant or summons for the probationer to appear. At the initial appearance, the court advises the probationer of the alleged violations and their rights, including the right to an attorney, the right to a hearing, and the right to present evidence and witnesses.

At the revocation hearing, the prosecution must prove the violation by a preponderance of the evidence—a significantly lower standard than the beyond a reasonable doubt standard required for criminal convictions. The rules of evidence are relaxed in revocation hearings, and hearsay evidence may be admitted. The probationer has the right to testify, present witnesses, and cross-examine the prosecution's witnesses.

If the court finds a violation occurred, it has broad discretion in determining the consequences. The court may continue probation with the same or modified conditions, extend the probation period, add conditions such as additional treatment or community service, impose a brief period of incarceration followed by continued probation, or revoke probation entirely and impose the original sentence up to the statutory maximum.

Defending Against Probation Violations

Effective defense against probation violations requires understanding both the facts of the alleged violation and the strategic options available. Common defenses include challenging whether the violation actually occurred—for example, contesting the reliability of drug test results, disputing the accuracy of probation officer reports, or demonstrating compliance with conditions. If the violation is a new criminal charge, challenging the underlying evidence can undermine the probation violation case even though the standard of proof is lower.

Even when a violation is established, mitigation is crucial. Courts consider the nature and seriousness of the violation, the probationer's overall compliance history, efforts to address underlying issues such as addiction or mental health, employment and family responsibilities, and the likelihood that continued probation will achieve its rehabilitative purpose. Our attorneys present compelling mitigating evidence to persuade courts that revocation is not the appropriate response to every violation.

Drug and Alcohol Testing Challenges

Failed drug or alcohol tests are among the most common probation violations. However, drug testing is not infallible. False positives can result from certain medications, foods, or supplements. Testing procedures may not have been followed correctly. Chain of custody issues can compromise the reliability of results. Our attorneys examine the testing methodology, laboratory procedures, and specific circumstances of each failed test to identify potential challenges.

In Western Colorado, where many individuals work in industries with irregular schedules—tourism, construction, agriculture—missed testing appointments may result from legitimate work conflicts rather than intentional avoidance. Presenting evidence of work schedules, communication with the probation officer, and good-faith efforts to comply can help avoid revocation for testing-related violations.

Deferred Judgment Revocations

A deferred judgment is a special form of probation where the defendant pleads guilty but the court defers entering a judgment of conviction. If the defendant successfully completes the deferred period, the case is dismissed and no conviction appears on their record. However, if the defendant violates the conditions of the deferred judgment, the court can revoke the deferral, enter the conviction, and impose any sentence authorized by law.

Deferred judgment revocations are particularly consequential because the defendant loses not only their freedom on probation but also the opportunity to avoid a criminal conviction. For individuals who accepted a deferred judgment specifically to protect their employment, professional licenses, or immigration status, revocation can be devastating. Our attorneys fight aggressively to preserve deferred judgments and the clean record they promise.

Intensive Supervision Probation

Intensive supervision probation (ISP) imposes more stringent conditions than standard probation, including more frequent reporting, increased drug testing, electronic monitoring, stricter curfews, and mandatory treatment participation. ISP is often imposed for serious offenses or when the court believes standard supervision is insufficient. Violations of ISP conditions are taken particularly seriously, and courts may be less inclined to continue probation after ISP violations.

Probation Violations in DUI Cases

DUI probation violations carry specific consequences under Colorado law. DUI probation typically includes mandatory alcohol education or therapy, installation of an ignition interlock device, community service, victim impact panels, and abstinence from alcohol. A probation violation in a DUI case—particularly a new alcohol-related offense or positive alcohol test—is viewed very seriously by courts. Our DUI Defense attorneys understand the specific requirements of DUI probation and defend clients against revocation in these cases.

The Importance of Prompt Action

If you know or suspect that you have violated a condition of probation, taking proactive steps before formal revocation proceedings can significantly impact the outcome. Contacting your probation officer to address the issue, voluntarily entering treatment, taking steps to cure the violation, and consulting with an attorney about your options can demonstrate good faith and may prevent the filing of a revocation complaint or result in more favorable treatment if proceedings are initiated.

Conversely, failing to address known violations, avoiding your probation officer, or absconding from supervision dramatically increases the likelihood of revocation and incarceration. Our attorneys help clients take appropriate proactive steps to protect their probation status.
