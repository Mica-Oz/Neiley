The consequences extend beyond criminal penalties—a conviction can affect your employment, professional licenses, housing, firearm rights, and reputation in the community. Degrees of Assault in Colorado

Colorado classifies assault into three degrees, each with distinct elements and penalties. Understanding the specific charge you face is the first step in building an effective defense.

Third-degree assault under C.R.S. § 18-3-204 is a class 1 misdemeanor involving knowingly or recklessly causing bodily injury to another person, or negligently causing bodily injury with a deadly weapon. This is the most common assault charge and carries penalties of up to 364 days in jail and fines up to $1,000. However, if the assault involves a peace officer, firefighter, or other protected class of victims, the charge can be elevated to a class 1 extraordinary risk misdemeanor with enhanced penalties of up to 24 months in jail.

Second-degree assault under C.R.S. § 18-3-203 is a class 4 felony involving intentionally causing serious bodily injury, causing bodily injury with a deadly weapon, recklessly causing serious bodily injury with a deadly weapon, or administering drugs or substances without consent. This charge carries a presumptive sentencing range of 2 to 6 years in the Department of Corrections, with mandatory parole. Second-degree assault is also charged when an individual causes injury to certain protected classes of victims such as police officers, judges, and emergency personnel.

First-degree assault under C.R.S. § 18-3-202 is a class 3 felony—one of the most serious violent crime charges in Colorado. It involves intentionally causing serious bodily injury with a deadly weapon, permanently disfiguring or disabling someone, or engaging in conduct creating a grave risk of death that causes serious bodily injury. First-degree assault carries a presumptive range of 4 to 12 years in the Department of Corrections, and if classified as a crime of violence, the court must impose a sentence to the Department of Corrections.

Related Charges

Assault charges often accompany or overlap with other criminal charges. Menacing under C.R.S. § 18-3-206 involves placing another person in fear of imminent serious bodily injury through threats or physical action. Simple menacing is a class 3 misdemeanor, but menacing with a deadly weapon—including a firearm—is a class 5 felony.

Harassment under C.R.S. § 18-9-111 can be charged when a person strikes, shoves, or kicks another person, or subjects them to physical contact intended to harass, annoy, or alarm. While typically a class 3 misdemeanor or petty offense, harassment charges can complicate an assault case and carry additional consequences including potential mandatory protection orders.

Reckless endangerment under C.R.S. § 18-3-208 involves recklessly engaging in conduct that creates a substantial risk of serious bodily injury to another person. This class 3 misdemeanor is sometimes charged alongside or as an alternative to assault charges.

Self-Defense and Affirmative Defenses

Colorado law recognizes several defenses to assault charges. Self-defense is the most common and powerful defense, codified under C.R.S. § 18-1-704. Colorado follows a "stand your ground" principle—there is no duty to retreat before using physical force in self-defense if you are in a place where you have a right to be. To establish self-defense, you must show that you reasonably believed physical force was necessary to defend yourself or another person from what you reasonably believed to be the imminent use of unlawful physical force.

The degree of force used must be proportional to the threat. Deadly force is justified only when you reasonably believe that a lesser degree of force would be inadequate and you have reasonable grounds to believe that you or another person is in imminent danger of being killed or receiving great bodily injury. Colorado's "Make My Day" law provides additional protections for the use of force against intruders in your dwelling.

Defense of others allows you to use physical force to defend a third party when you reasonably believe that person is in imminent danger. Defense of property permits reasonable force to prevent theft or criminal mischief, though the use of deadly force to protect property alone is generally not justified. Consent can also be a defense in limited circumstances, such as injuries sustained during contact sports or other consensual activities.

Assault in the Context of Domestic Violence

When an assault occurs between individuals in an intimate relationship, the charge carries a domestic violence sentence enhancer under C.R.S. § 18-6-801. This enhancer does not change the underlying charge but adds mandatory conditions including domestic violence treatment programs, mandatory protection orders, and potential loss of firearm rights. A domestic violence designation also means the case cannot be plea-bargained to a non-domestic violence charge without the prosecutor's consent and court approval. Our domestic violence defense page provides more detailed information about these proceedings.

Assault Involving Alcohol and Nightlife

In Western Colorado's mountain resort communities, a significant number of assault cases arise in connection with alcohol consumption at bars, restaurants, and special events. Telluride and Aspen host numerous festivals, concerts, and events where alcohol-fueled confrontations can escalate quickly. What begins as a verbal argument can lead to assault charges that carry lasting consequences.

These cases present unique defense opportunities. Witness accounts in bar fight situations are often unreliable due to alcohol consumption, poor lighting, and chaotic conditions. Surveillance footage may tell a different story than the initial police report. Identifying the initial aggressor can be difficult when multiple people are involved. Our attorneys thoroughly investigate the circumstances of each incident, interview witnesses, obtain video evidence, and reconstruct events to present the most favorable defense.

Penalties and Consequences

The penalties for assault conviction in Colorado depend on the degree of the charge, prior criminal history, and whether sentencing enhancers apply. Beyond incarceration and fines, an assault conviction can result in mandatory anger management or domestic violence treatment programs, restitution to the victim for medical expenses and other losses, probation with strict conditions, loss of the right to possess firearms, a permanent criminal record that appears on background checks, immigration consequences for non-citizens, and difficulty finding employment, housing, and educational opportunities.

For felony assault convictions classified as crimes of violence, the court must sentence the defendant to the Department of Corrections, and probation is not an option. The mandatory minimum sentence is the midpoint of the presumptive range, and the court can impose up to twice the maximum of the presumptive range.

Building Your Defense

An effective assault defense requires prompt, thorough investigation and strategic planning. Critical steps include preserving and obtaining evidence such as surveillance video, photographs, and medical records before they are lost or destroyed, identifying and interviewing witnesses while memories are fresh, analyzing the police investigation for procedural errors or constitutional violations, evaluating the alleged victim's credibility and motivations, examining forensic evidence including injury patterns and medical reports, and assessing whether plea negotiations or trial present the best path to a favorable outcome.

Every assault case is different, and the defense strategy must be tailored to the specific facts and circumstances. What works in one case may not apply in another. Our attorneys take the time to understand every aspect of your situation and develop a defense approach designed to achieve the best possible result.

Misdemeanor vs. Felony Assault

The distinction between misdemeanor and felony assault often comes down to the severity of injury and the means used. "Bodily injury" and "serious bodily injury" are legally distinct concepts in Colorado. Bodily injury includes physical pain, illness, or impairment. Serious bodily injury involves a substantial risk of death, permanent disfigurement, prolonged loss of any bodily function, or fractures. The classification of injuries can determine whether you face months in county jail or years in state prison.

Our attorneys carefully scrutinize the evidence regarding the nature and extent of injuries. Medical records, expert testimony, and independent medical evaluation can all be used to challenge the prosecution's characterization of injuries and potentially reduce the severity of charges.
