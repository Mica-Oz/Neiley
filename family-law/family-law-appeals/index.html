Family Law Appeals in Colorado

When a Colorado district court enters an order that is wrong on the law, unsupported by evidence, or otherwise unjust, the affected party has the right to appeal. Family law appeals are heard by the Colorado Court of Appeals and, in exceptional cases, by the Colorado Supreme Court. These appeals are very different from trial court litigation—they are governed by specialized procedures, require briefing and oral argument rather than evidence presentation, and focus on legal analysis of the trial court record. At Neiley & Morris, we represent parties in family law appeals throughout Colorado, including those originating from Telluride, Aspen, and Rifle.

An appeal is not a do-over of the trial. The appellate court does not hear new evidence, does not re-weigh the evidence presented at trial, and does not substitute its judgment for the trial court's on factual matters. Instead, the appellate court reviews the trial court's record for legal errors and abuses of discretion. Understanding what can—and cannot—be addressed on appeal is essential to evaluating whether an appeal is worth pursuing.

What Can Be Appealed

Most final orders in family law cases are appealable, including:

  • Final dissolution decrees and the orders that accompany them (property division, spousal maintenance, parenting plans, child support)
  • Final orders allocating parental responsibilities in stand-alone APR cases
  • Final orders modifying parenting time, decision-making, child support, or maintenance
  • Orders on contempt of court
  • Orders on attorney's fees
  • Orders entering or denying civil protection orders
  • Certain other orders that constitute final judgments under Colorado law

Some orders are interlocutory and cannot be immediately appealed—they must wait until the end of the case. Identifying whether an order is appealable, and meeting the strict timing requirements for filing an appeal, is critical and time-sensitive.

Standards of Review

Appellate courts apply different standards of review depending on the nature of the issue being appealed. Understanding these standards is essential to evaluating the strength of an appeal.

De novo review applies to questions of law, including the interpretation of statutes and constitutional questions. The appellate court gives no deference to the trial court's legal conclusions and decides the legal issue afresh. This is the most favorable standard for appellants.

Abuse of discretion review applies to most discretionary decisions in family law, including custody determinations, property division, and maintenance awards. The appellate court will reverse only if the trial court's decision is manifestly arbitrary, unreasonable, or unfair. This is a deferential standard that is difficult to meet.

Clear error review applies to factual findings. The appellate court will reverse only if the findings are clearly erroneous—essentially unsupported by any evidence in the record. This is the most deferential standard.

The applicable standard often determines the likelihood of success on appeal. Issues subject to de novo review have a much higher chance of success than issues subject to abuse of discretion or clear error review.

Common Grounds for Family Law Appeals

The most common issues in family law appeals include:

  • Legal errors: The trial court applied the wrong legal standard, misinterpreted a statute, or failed to make required findings
  • Property division errors: The court mischaracterized property as marital or separate, used improper valuation methods, or failed to account for all assets
  • Maintenance errors: The court miscalculated income, applied the wrong guideline, or failed to consider required factors
  • Custody errors: The court applied the wrong standard for modification or failed to make findings on the best interests factors
  • Child support errors: The court used incorrect income figures or failed to apply guideline factors properly
  • Procedural errors: Due process violations, improper exclusion of evidence, or other procedural irregularities
  • Insufficient findings: The court failed to make findings of fact and conclusions of law adequate to permit appellate review

The Appellate Process

Colorado family law appeals begin with a notice of appeal that must be filed within 49 days after the entry of the order being appealed. This deadline is strict and rarely extended. Missing the deadline forfeits the right to appeal.

After the notice of appeal is filed, the parties prepare and file the record on appeal, which includes the trial court file and any necessary transcripts of hearings. The appellant then files an opening brief that identifies the issues, presents the arguments, and cites the legal authorities. The appellee files an answer brief, and the appellant may file a reply brief. In some cases, the court schedules oral argument; in others, the court decides the appeal on the briefs alone.

The Court of Appeals then issues a written decision that may affirm, reverse, or modify the trial court's order. If the order is reversed, the case is typically remanded to the trial court for further proceedings consistent with the appellate decision. The losing party may petition the Colorado Supreme Court for further review, but the Supreme Court accepts only a small percentage of cases.

Effect of Appeal on Trial Court Orders

Filing an appeal does not automatically stay or suspend the trial court's order. The order remains in full force and effect during the appeal unless the appellant obtains a stay from the trial court or the appellate court. Stays are not granted as a matter of right and require a showing of likelihood of success on appeal, irreparable harm without a stay, balance of hardships, and the public interest.

This means that during a custody appeal, the existing custody order continues to govern. During a maintenance appeal, the existing maintenance order continues to require payment. Parties who hope to change the status quo while the appeal is pending must specifically seek a stay.

Cost-Benefit Analysis

Appeals are expensive and time-consuming. The appellate process typically takes a year or more from the notice of appeal to the final decision. The cost depends on the complexity of the issues and the length of the trial court record. Before pursuing an appeal, clients should carefully evaluate the strength of the legal issues, the probability of success, the potential outcome if successful, and the costs involved.

We provide candid assessments of appellate prospects so clients can make informed decisions about whether to appeal. In some cases, an appeal is clearly warranted. In others, the better course is to accept the trial court's decision and move forward.
